What is the churn model?

The companion notebook builds a machine-learning model that predicts which Medicare patients attributed to an ACO are about to stop receiving care in the network. It uses 15 patient-level features (visit history, PCP relationship strength, out-of-network trends, tenure) to assign each patient a risk score between 0 and 1.

Patients are then sorted into Low, Medium, and High risk tiers. The model flags 74 high-risk patients who are most likely to churn without intervention. The rest of this brief explains why those specific signals are predictive, using recent peer-reviewed research and government data.

§ 01 Context

What is an ACO, and why does churn hurt?

An Accountable Care Organization is a group of doctors and hospitals that shares responsibility for the cost and quality of care for a group of Medicare patients. When the ACO spends less than Medicare's benchmark and hits quality targets, it earns a share of the savings. In 2024, 476 ACOs covering 10.3 million Medicare beneficiaries generated $2.4 billion in net savings, a record for the Medicare Shared Savings Program.1

The program works only if the ACO can keep patients engaged with its providers. Medicare beneficiaries, however, can see any provider they want. The attributed patient panel shifts every quarter based on where care actually happens.

“The open Medicare network can result in significant patient churn each quarter.” CareJourney analysis of ACO attribution patterns6

Program-level data shows the scale of the instability. A 2024 analysis in JAMA Network Open found a 16% annual clinician exit rate among ACOs that remained in the MSSP, and that only half of ACOs stayed in the program for two full contract cycles (six years).2 When clinicians leave, most of their attributed beneficiaries leave with them.3

The financial stakes are meaningful at the ACO level. CBO projects that even modest changes to attribution stability affect billions in program savings over a decade.4 The 2024 Health Affairs analysis of MSSP performance notes that among ACOs subject to new benchmarking policies, per-capita savings dropped to $143, compared to $241 for ACOs not subject to the new methodology.5 Retention directly affects which side of that line an ACO ends up on.

On the synthetic dataset. The notebook uses synthetic data calibrated to published patterns, including an 8% inactivity churn rate, 30 to 40% out-of-network utilization among churners, and higher churn in newer members. It is not claiming to match a specific ACO, only to sit within ranges the research reports.
§ 03 The signals

Why these four features carry the signal

The model uses 15 features, but four do most of the work. Each one traces back to a specific research finding.

01

Recent visit activity

Churned patients in the test set average 172 days since their last visit; active patients average 43. This aligns with the MSSP's operational attribution methodology, which keys off continuous primary care usage within a measurement window.7

Tenure in the ACO

New members are disproportionately vulnerable. Research on ACO network comprehensiveness shows patient panel stability is associated with better disease management outcomes, and that engagement patterns set in the first months after attribution tend to persist.8

PCP engagement

Beneficiaries in the highest continuity-of-care quintile have 16.1% lower odds of hospitalization than those in the lowest, per a Medicare claims study of 1.4 million beneficiaries.9 A 2022 Health Services Research paper found every 0.1 increase in physician continuity score is associated with $151 lower expenditure per beneficiary per year.10

Out-of-network trend

A rising share of visits happening outside the ACO. Medically complex patients are the most likely to receive care outside the network, compounding the cost impact.11 Once patients establish care elsewhere, the relationship is often already lost.

§ 04 What works

What actually prevents churn

Annual Wellness Visits

Free yearly preventive checkups. They reinforce the PCP relationship, close quality-measure gaps, and strengthen attribution. A 2026 AJMC analysis found Medicare beneficiaries in two-sided risk ACOs had 65% AWV completion in 2024, compared to 42% in non-ACO Medicare.12 A peer-reviewed study of two Aledade-partnered PCP-led ACOs specifically examined AWVs' association with quality and cost outcomes using MSSP claims data.13

Network management

The CBO's 2024 review of Medicare ACOs notes that reducing leakage is one of the operational levers most strongly associated with financial performance, alongside high-touch care coordination for medically complex patients.4 This is primarily a workflow problem rather than a clinical one.

§ 05 Caveats

What the model does, and does not do

It is a Random Forest classifier. Hundreds of decision trees vote, and the model averages their answers into a probability between 0 and 1. High scores go into the intervention queue.

It does not infer causation. The model matches patterns. Whether a feature causes churn or merely correlates with it is a separate question requiring different methods.

It does not pick the intervention. A high-risk flag tells the care team that a patient is drifting. Whether a phone call, transportation support, or PCP outreach works best is what an A/B test would answer.

On the 98.5% accuracy. Performance on synthetic data is easier to achieve than on real claims, because the data generator encodes the patterns the model is asked to detect. Real data has more noise, missingness, and confounders. The purpose of the synthetic exercise is to demonstrate the workflow: feature engineering, risk tiering, and translation into action.
