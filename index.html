Background

What's an ACO, and why does churn matter?

An Accountable Care Organization (ACO) is a group of doctors, hospitals, and clinics that voluntarily team up to deliver coordinated care to Medicare patients. When they keep patients healthy and spending below a government-set target (called a "benchmark"), they earn shared savings. When they overspend, they face penalties.

The Medicare Shared Savings Program now covers over 10 million beneficiaries across 480 ACOs and set a record $2.4 billion in savings for Medicare in 2024. But keeping practices in the program is a persistent challenge — when a practice leaves, the ACO loses attributed patients, disrupts care coordination, and jeopardizes its cost targets.

Real-world context

A 2025 study in Health Affairs Scholar found clinicians in the MSSP had a 16% annual exit rate, with the average clinician staying just 3.6 years. Only 23% of clinicians who joined before 2013 were still in the program 9 years later. [1]

This project builds a machine learning system to flag practices likely to leave — before they do — giving ACO leadership time to intervene.

The Data

What we modeled

Six interconnected data tables capture the full picture of an ACO practice: financial performance, patient risk profiles, operational capabilities, and regional market dynamics. Because real ACO data is protected health information, a realistic synthetic dataset was generated using known ACO churn dynamics from peer-reviewed literature.

Research backing

When CMS introduced the "Pathways to Success" rule requiring downside risk, more ACOs exited the MSSP than joined — for the first time in program history. [3] A separate study found nearly two-thirds of small rural ACOs exited when required to assume downside risk. [4]

A t-test confirmed the financial gap is statistically significant: practices that churned had benchmark gaps averaging +7.7% vs −1.2% for retained practices — nearly a 9-point difference (p < 0.001).

Key Findings

Top predictors of churn

Random Forest feature importance and SHAP analysis agreed on the ranking. Financial factors dominate — but operational gaps matter too.

1
Benchmark gap
Spending more than the government's target is the single biggest red flag. Practices over 5% above benchmark face extreme churn risk.
2
Shared savings
Practices earning savings stay. Financial reward is the strongest retention lever available to ACO leadership.
3
Net financial result
The real "take-home" from the ACO model — shared savings minus any penalties paid back to CMS.
4
Staff ratio
Practices with fewer support staff per provider struggle to coordinate care and manage complex, high-cost patients.
5
Cost trend
Rising year-over-year costs signal incoming financial distress — an early warning before losses officially hit.
6
EHR capability score
Practices with weak EHR systems can't track quality metrics or identify high-risk patients before they escalate to costly care.
7
Years in ACO
Newer practices churn most. Those with more than 5 years in the program are significantly more stable and invested.
Machine Learning

How the models performed

An ROC-AUC of 0.90 is considered excellent. In plain terms: if you randomly pick one practice that will churn and one that won't, the model correctly identifies which is which 90% of the time. Out of 258 churners in the test set, the model flagged 225 correctly — giving the ACO team time to act.

Survival analysis

Kaplan-Meier survival curves confirmed that practices earning shared savings retained significantly better over time. A log-rank test showed the difference is statistically significant (p < 0.0001). Practices on downside risk tracks also showed materially worse retention curves.

What if we changed something?

Recommendations

What should ACO leadership actually do?

  • 1
    Deploy the model in production (via MLflow or Unity Catalog) to score every practice monthly. Flag any practice with predicted churn probability above 60% for proactive outreach — before they've decided to leave.
  • 2
    Advocate with CMS for benchmark recalibration — particularly for practices serving high-risk patient populations. This is the single highest-impact lever. Even a 2% improvement could retain nearly 400 practices annually.
  • 3
    Invest upfront in small practice infrastructure: care coordination staff, EHR upgrades, and population health analytics. New practices churn most — and respond best to early support.
  • 4
    Tailor interventions by segment. Struggling practices need financial rescue. Mixed-performance practices need coaching. Strong performers need recognition and contract incentives to stay.
  • 5
    Track leading indicators monthly: benchmark gap trends, cost trend direction, and the share of practices earning positive shared savings. Don't wait for year-end reconciliation to discover a practice is in trouble.
